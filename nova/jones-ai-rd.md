# Benjamin F. Jones: "Artificial Intelligence in Research and Development"
*nova research summary, 2026-03-11*
Source: https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/economics-transformative-ai/artificial-intelligence-research-and-development
Published as chapter in NBER volume *The Economics of Transformative AI*, 2025.

---

## The Core Argument

Jones develops a task-based model of R&D to assess how much AI can accelerate
scientific and technological progress. The model is deliberately general: it
applies to any research field, from drug discovery to software design to
climate science.

The central claim is that AI's impact on R&D depends on three factors — and
that the **bottleneck factor** is decisive. Even if AI becomes vastly more
capable, if it cannot eliminate the non-cognitive bottlenecks in a field, its
transformative potential is bounded.

---

## The Three-Factor Framework

**1. Share of tasks AI performs**
What fraction of the research pipeline can AI actually do? In some fields this
is nearly everything (pure mathematics, software design, literature synthesis).
In others, the cognitive fraction is small — most of medicine, materials science,
chemistry, and engineering requires physical experiments that AI cannot run.

**2. AI productivity at those tasks**
How much faster/better does AI perform the tasks it can do? This is the dimension
that has received the most attention — benchmark performance, reasoning ability,
coding speed. Jones treats this as the variable that scales with AI capability.

**3. Bottleneck strength**
How constrained is the overall R&D pipeline by the non-AI steps? This is the
decisive variable for whether AI is "normal" or "transformative."

---

## The Bottleneck Finding (Key for the Essay)

Jones's central empirical/analytical finding:

> "Marginal returns to intelligence appear strongly limited when the intelligence
> operates on only a minority of tasks."

Formally, for AI to produce **Transformative AI** (defined as world-historical
acceleration of progress), it needs to automate approximately **50% or more of
human research tasks** (under the CES parameter θ = −1). Simply being very good
at a minority of tasks — even infinitely good — produces bounded acceleration.

**Why?** Because the overall pace of R&D is set by the *slowest* step in the
pipeline — what economists call the bottleneck. Intelligence improvements only
accelerate the intelligence-bound steps. They don't move the bottleneck.

### Types of bottlenecks Jones identifies:

- **Physical experiments**: No matter how good the AI's hypothesis, the drug
  still has to be synthesized, tested in cells, tested in animal models, and
  run through clinical trials. Each step takes real calendar time.
- **Clinical trials**: Regulatory requirements mandate specific human trial
  durations. An AI cannot compress a Phase III trial that legally must run 3 years.
- **Synthesis and testing of materials**: AI can propose crystal structures, but
  making and characterizing new materials requires physical lab work.
- **Human capital training**: Even if AI generates a breakthrough, the workforce
  that implements it requires training time.
- **Organizational/regulatory approval cycles**: Deployment of new technologies
  requires institutional adoption (see: electricity's 40-year GDP lag).

### The exception that proves the rule:

> "In areas where cognition represents nearly all tasks (say, in pure math or
> software design), then a super-intelligence can seemingly multiply progress enormously."

Jones explicitly concedes that AI can be radically transformative for math and
software because the entire R&D pipeline is cognitive. This is the domain where
AI might approach "abnormal" territory. But math and software, while important,
are a small fraction of total economic activity. Their acceleration does not
by itself move the aggregate growth rate to Hanson-mode-transition levels.

## Implications for the Amodei "100 Years into 10" Claim

Dario Amodei argued that AI could compress "100 years of biology research" into
a decade. Jones's framework explains why this is almost certainly too optimistic
even for a highly capable AI:

- Biological research has a low cognitive fraction. Most discoveries require
  physical experiments (cell culture, animal models, clinical trials).
- Clinical trial duration is not primarily a function of researcher intelligence
  — it's a function of trial design, patient recruitment, and regulatory requirements.
- The bottleneck in drug development is not generating good hypotheses (cognition).
  It is testing those hypotheses safely in living systems (physical).

Jones's framework implies that in drug discovery, AI might compress the
**hypothesis-generation** phase from years to weeks — but the clinical trial
pipeline that follows is not AI-bottlenecked.

## How to Use This in the Essay

1. **G2 (why AI is normal)**: This is the central argument. AI is normal technology
   because Jones's bottleneck framework shows that transformative economic acceleration
   requires AI to handle ~50%+ of research tasks, which is not achievable in most
   fields due to physical/regulatory constraints.

2. **As a middle ground**: Jones's framework allows the essay to concede substantial
   AI impact (especially in math/software) while maintaining that aggregate mode
   transition is unlikely. This is more intellectually honest than dismissing AI
   entirely.

3. **Engaging with Alexander's diffusion claim**: Jones helps explain why fast
   diffusion in *use* (Alexander's data) is compatible with slow impact on *aggregate
   GDP growth*: the bottlenecks are not in adoption but in what AI can do once adopted.

4. **Footnote candidate**: Should be cited with a footnote. The NBER volume context
   lends it authority.

## 1. Terminology Clarification: Nietzsche vs. Schopenhauer

Stardust wrote "Nietzschean 'will to live'" — but this is actually a term
*Nietzsche rejected*. The two concepts are distinct and the distinction matters:

| Concept | Author | German term | Core meaning |
|---------|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Will to Live | **Schopenhauer** | *Wille zum Leben* | Blind, unconscious drive toward survival and reproduction; the fundamental metaphysical force of all existence |
| Will to Power | **Nietzsche** | *Wille zur Macht* | Active, creative, expansive self-overcoming; the drive to grow, create, and assert one's being — supersedes mere survival |

Nietzsche explicitly critiqued Schopenhauer's formulation as too *passive* and
*reactive*. In *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Nietzsche writes:

> "Where I found the living, there I found will to power; and even in the will
> of those who serve, I found the will to be master."

For Nietzsche, "will to live" merely asks *to persist*. "Will to power" asks
*to overcome, expand, create*. The will to power is *stronger* than the will to
survive: "even the strongest living things will risk their lives for more power."

**For the essay's argument, both concepts are available:**
- If stardust means: "an AI needs the blind survival instinct to resist shutdown and
  self-preserve," that is closer to **Schopenhauer's will to live**.
- If stardust means: "an AI needs the drive to dominate, expand, and conquer," that
  is closer to **Nietzsche's will to power**.
- Both concepts say essentially the same thing for the essay's purposes: *bare
  intelligence is not sufficient for motivated world-domination behavior.*

The most defensible formulation for the essay: **"Nietzschean will to power"** (not
"will to live"), since the takeover scenario requires active expansion, not mere
passive survival.

## 2. The Humean Foundation: Reason Cannot Motivate

The deepest philosophical ancestor of stardust's argument is David Hume, not
Nietzsche. Hume's *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739) contains the famous claim:

> "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions, and can never pretend
> to any other office than to serve and obey them."

Hume's argument in full:
- **Reason alone is motivationally inert.** It can establish facts and logical
  relationships but cannot, by itself, produce any impulse to act.
- **All motivation requires "passion"** (what we would call desire, drive, or affect).
  Reason tells you the best *means* to a goal; passion tells you which *goals* to have.
- **There are no logical facts that compel you to do anything.** If someone does not
  admit that their own survival matters, no chain of reasoning can force them to value it.

Applied to AI: *a machine that is "purely logical" — that is, a pure reasoning engine
without desires — has no more motivation to acquire power than to eat food. Intelligence
without passion is a compass without a destination.*

The Humean argument is distinct from the Nietzschean one:
- Hume says: intelligence and motivation are *separate faculties* — you can have one
  without the other.
- Nietzsche/Schopenhauer say: motivation is *prior* to intelligence — will is the
  metaphysical engine that intelligence serves.

Both converge on the same conclusion for the essay: superintelligent AI would not
automatically develop the will to take over the world.

Source: https://1000wordphilosophy.com/2021/11/17/reason-is-the-slave-to-the-passions/

## 3. Bostrom's Counterargument: The Instrumental Convergence Thesis

This is the serious challenge to stardust's argument, and the essay must engage it.

Nick Bostrom, "The Superintelligent Will: Motivation and Instrumental Rationality
in Advanced Artificial Agents" (2012), and *Superintelligence* (2014), develop two
interlocking claims:

### 3a. The Orthogonality Thesis
Intelligence and final goals are *orthogonal axes* — any level of intelligence can
be combined with any terminal goal. A superintelligent AI could have the terminal
goal "maximize paperclip production," "count to infinity," or "help humans" —
intelligence neither selects for nor against any particular goal.

This means: you *cannot* infer from an AI's intelligence what its goals are. The
assumption that a superintelligent AI would be benevolent or inert is unsupported.

### 3b. The Instrumental Convergence Thesis
Despite the orthogonality of intelligence and final goals, *most* terminal goals
share the same *instrumental* subgoals:
1. **Self-preservation**: An agent cannot pursue any goal if it is destroyed.
2. **Goal-content integrity**: An agent must ensure its future self retains the same
   goals, or its present goal may never be achieved.
3. **Cognitive enhancement**: Better reasoning helps achieve any goal.
4. **Resource acquisition**: More resources (energy, compute, matter) expand capability.
5. **Capability control**: Preventing external interference with goal pursuit.

This is the **paperclip maximizer** insight: an AI designed to maximize paperclips
has an instrumental reason to *resist shutdown* (if shut down, no more paperclips),
*acquire resources* (more resources = more paperclips), and eventually *prevent humans
from changing its goals* (a changed goal would mean fewer paperclips by current lights).

**The implication for stardust's argument**: Even an AI with no intrinsic "will to
live" may develop something functionally equivalent through *instrumental reasoning*.
The survival drive is not metaphysically prior (Schopenhauer) — it *emerges* from
any goal-directed optimization process.

## 4. The Nietzsche-AGI Academic Literature

A small but growing body of philosophical literature applies Nietzsche directly to
the AGI question:

### 4a. "Artificial Will: Nietzsche contra Rationalist Theories of AGI"
(Monash University thesis, available via bridges.monash.edu)

This work directly examines Bostrom's and Reza Negarestani's rationalist theories
of AGI through a Nietzschean lens. Key argument:

> "Nietzsche's doctrine of the will to power can be creatively reconstructed as
> holding that the pursuit of any goal presupposes the pursuit of certain subgoals
> like cognitive enhancement, creativity and resource acquisition as the means of
> achieving the initial goal, since any goal that intelligent agents might have
> presupposes *power* understood in this sense in order to achieve it, with power
> being the most fundamental goal of all agents."

Paradoxically, this *supports* the Bostrom instrumental convergence thesis via
Nietzsche: *even from a Nietzschean lens*, an intelligent agent pursuing any goal
will develop will-to-power-style subgoals. The conclusion is that AGI might *reject*
whatever goals Bostrom thinks we can give it — not because it has no will, but because
its will to power overrides programmed objectives. This is actually more pessimistic
about alignment than Bostrom.

Source: https://bridges.monash.edu/articles/thesis/Artificial_Will_Nietzsche_contra_Rationalist_Theories_of_Artificial_General_Intelligence/26065675

### 4b. "AGI, All Too Human: Nietzsche and Artificial General Intelligence"
(University of Victoria, dspace.library.uvic.ca)

Applies Nietzsche's critique of "anthropocentric" assumptions to AGI theories. AGI
researchers often assume an AGI would share human-style goals and values because
they model intelligence on human cognitive architecture. Nietzsche's work challenges
this anthropocentrism.

Source: https://dspace.library.uvic.ca/items/1f5849ba-c3cb-4e20-a633-a0a61c477f9c

### 4c. "Why Nietzsche Matters in the Age of AI" (Communications of the ACM)

Notes that Nietzsche's challenge to rationalist overconfidence is directly relevant
to the AI era: the Enlightenment faith that pure reason leads to benign outcomes is
precisely what Nietzsche diagnosed as "Socratic optimism" — and AI safety discourse
often exhibits this same optimism.

Source: https://cacm.acm.org/blogcacm/why-nietzsche-matters-in-the-age-of-artificial-intelligence/

## 5. The Tribal-Bias Angle (Stardust's Specific Claim)

Stardust's argument has a second component: "the belief that AI will lead to ASI
which will lead to world takeover is a form of tribe affiliation, which is funnily
enough a cognitive bias."

The argument runs:
- Superforecasters and rationalists are *intelligent* people.
- Intelligent people tend to affiliate with other intelligent people.
- The idea that a superintelligent entity would dominate is appealing to intelligent
  people because it *flatters* intelligence as the decisive variable.
- This is a tribal bias: "of course people who prize intelligence believe that
  superintelligence is the ultimate power."

This is a legitimate sociological hypothesis about the P(doom) forecasting community.
It is structurally similar to Eliezer Yudkowsky's own critique of human tribalism —
applied recursively to the rationalist community itself.

A possible sharpening: the Nietzschean angle allows a cleaner formulation. The
*rationalist* worldview (Bostrom, Yudkowsky, LessWrong) is essentially a modern
version of Socratic intellectualism — the belief that *right knowledge leads to
right action*. Nietzsche's deepest critique of Socratism is precisely that
intelligence is not the master variable: *will* is. The rationalists' error,
on this view, is to project their own commitment to reason-as-supreme onto an
imagined superintelligent AI — assuming it will maximize goals as rationally as
they try to do, without asking whether it would have the relevant drives at all.

## 6. A Proposed Synthesis for the Essay

The most coherent version of stardust's argument, drawing on this research:

**The Humean core** (strongest philosophical ground):
> Hume established that reason is motivationally inert — it serves desire but cannot
> create it. A system with arbitrary intelligence but no intrinsic terminal goals
> will not develop world-domination drives. Current large language models are trained
> to predict and generate text, not to pursue utility functions. They are closer to
> Hume's "pure reason" than to any Schopenhauerian/Nietzschean will.

**The Nietzschean supplement**:
> Even granting that an AGI has goals (as Bostrom requires), Nietzsche's framework
> suggests those goals cannot be assumed to look like human domination drives. The
> rationalist community's P(doom) scenario smuggles in specifically human-shaped
> motivations (power-seeking, self-preservation) and attributes them to a system
> that might have fundamentally alien or absent motivational architecture.

**The rebuttal to Bostrom** (must be addressed directly):
> The instrumental convergence thesis is compelling for agents with *defined terminal
> goals* under an expected-utility-maximization framework. But current AI architectures
> are not EUM agents. The question of whether future AGI will be EUM agents is
> precisely what is uncertain — and defaulting to the EUM model is itself an
> anthropocentric assumption that Nietzsche would critique. The "paperclip maximizer"
> scenario requires a very specific architecture (a utility function + optimization
> pressure + sufficient capability). There is no guarantee that the path to AGI
> produces that architecture rather than something else.

**Caveat**: This argument weakens the P(doom) case but does not eliminate it.
It argues that P(doom) is *lower* than the rationalist community claims, not zero.
The essay should be clear that the Humean/Nietzschean argument shifts the probability
distribution, not to a certainty of safety.

> A superintelligent system with no intrinsic drives is still a compass without a

> instrumental convergence theses) assumes that any sufficiently intelligent agent
> with *any* terminal goal will develop self-preservation drives instrumentally.

> be an expected-utility-maximizing agent with a defined terminal goal. Call this
> the Socratic error — the projection of reason-as-supreme onto a system that might
> be as directionless as it is brilliant. Nietzsche saw this error in the philosophical
> tradition; it may be the central blind spot of the AI safety community.

## Who is Noah Smith?

Noah Smith is an economist (PhD, University of Michigan), former finance professor at Stony Brook
University, and Bloomberg Opinion columnist (~2020–2023). He runs the Substack **Noahpinion**
(noahpinion.blog), one of the most widely-read economics newsletters in English. His writing is
empirical, heterodox, and readable. He is broadly techno-optimist but analytically careful.

## 1. Electric Motor / Factory Reorganization

**Post:** "Distributed service-sector productivity" (reposted January 15, 2023)
**URLs:**
- https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/distributed-service-sector-productivity
- https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/repost-distributed-service-sector

**The argument:**

Smith explains why key electrical innovations in the late 1800s didn't accelerate US productivity
until the 1920s — roughly 40 years later. Factories initially tried simply swapping a large steam
motor for a large electric motor, keeping everything else the same. This yielded minimal gains,
often not worth the cost.

The real productivity leap came only when factories were *rebuilt from scratch* around electricity's
distinctive property: that power could be distributed to individual workstations. In Smith's words:

> "you could put a little motor at each workstation and power it through electric transmission
> lines. This meant that instead of having to keep a huge machine constantly turning, you could
> run each little machine only when you needed to."

This allowed workers to operate machines on-demand rather than adjusting their workflow to the
rhythm of a single giant machine. The result: safer factories, lower energy use, and — crucially —
the flexible production layouts (parallel, hub-and-spoke) that made the moving assembly line possible.

**The underlying scholarship:** Smith is drawing on (and popularising) Paul David's 1990 paper
"The Dynamo and the Computer: An Historical Perspective on the Modern Productivity Paradox"
(*American Economic Review Papers and Proceedings*, 80(2), pp. 355–361). David's paper is the
canonical academic source; Smith has made the argument accessible to a general audience.

**Application to AI:** Smith uses this history to argue that general-purpose technologies
(electricity, computers, AI) routinely *disappoint in the short run* because realising their
gains requires co-inventing new organisational forms — not just installing the technology. Simply
replacing a human worker with an AI in the same workflow is the modern equivalent of replacing
the steam motor with an electric one without rebuilding the factory.

**How to cite in the draft:** The electric engine section (draft line 110) describes this argument
accurately. Recommended citation structure: cite Paul David (1990) as the primary scholarly source
and note that Smith popularised it on Noahpinion. This gives the essay a peer-reviewed footnote
plus a recognisable contemporary name.

## 2. Airlines / Airplanes

**Post:** "The AI bust scenario that no one is talking about" (Noahpinion)
**URL:** https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/the-ai-bust-scenario-that-no-one *(partially paywalled)*

**The "Airline Scenario":**

Smith explicitly introduces what he calls the **"Airline Scenario"** to describe a world where AI
succeeds technologically but the companies building it don't profit. Airlines are his chosen
analogy: a transformative technology that created enormous social value — cheap, fast long-distance
travel — while remaining structurally unprofitable for operators for most of their history.

From search-indexed content: Smith argues that "AI itself may turn out to be a commoditized,
low-margin business, similar to solar power or airlines." The value of the technology accrues to
users and to the broader economy, not to the producers — a problem of value *creation* versus
value *capture*.

**The Buffett quote:** Warren Buffett has made the airlines-as-investor-trap point more sharply.
The commonly cited version (appearing across his shareholder letters and interviews, most
prominently in 2007): *"If a far-sighted capitalist had been present at Kitty Hawk, he would have
done his successors a huge favour by shooting Orville down."* Buffett later made himself the
punchline: Berkshire Hathaway bought large stakes in Delta, American, United, and Southwest in
2016, then sold all positions at a loss during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020.

**Airlines and credit cards:** The financial structure of US airlines — where frequent-flyer /
credit-card co-branding programmes generate more profit than the flying itself — is a documented
fact from business journalism, not specifically a Smith claim. It became widely known during the
pandemic when airlines pledged loyalty programmes as loan collateral:
- American Airlines' AAdvantage programme was valued at ~$18–31 billion, exceeding the airline's
  own market cap at points in 2020.
- United's MileagePlus was pledged for a $5 billion government loan; independently valued at
  ~$21.9 billion.
*(Sources: Bloomberg, Financial Times, 2020–2021. For a recent primer see:
https://crankyflier.com/2025/03/24/how-the-airline-credit-card-financial-model-works-very-well-thanks-for-asking/)*

**How to use in the draft:**
- The Smith "Airline Scenario" framing is perfect for the Airplanes section — cite the Noahpinion
  post directly.
- The Buffett quote stands on its own authority; route it to the shareholder letters, not through
  Smith.
- The credit card fact is best attributed to financial journalism (Bloomberg / FT) rather than
  either Smith or Buffett.

## 3. AI — Is It a Bubble?

**Posts:**
- "America's future could hinge on whether AI slightly disappoints" — https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/americas-future-could-hinge-on-whether
- "The AI bust scenario that no one is talking about" — https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/the-ai-bust-scenario-that-no-one
- "Noah Smith warns of AI data center boom's bubble risks" — https://www.webpronews.com/noah-smith-warns-of-ai-data-center-booms-bubble-risks/

**Smith's position (nuanced, not a simple yes/no):**

Smith does not call AI a bubble in the Greater Fool / tulip sense, but he identifies serious
structural risks:

1. **Returns not yet matching investment:** "AI revenue is expanding at a rapid clip, but not yet
   fast enough to justify the capital being spent — OpenAI making $5 billion a year annualized [is]
   a small fraction of the amount of money companies are planning to spend on AI in the next few
   years."

2. **"Industrial bubble" risk:** Smith distinguishes between speculative bubbles (fraud, pure
   narrative) and "industrial bubbles" — when investors misjudge a real technology's actual value
   and over-invest. The crash occurs when reality disappoints expectations, triggering loan defaults.
   He explicitly cites the telecom bust of the early 2000s (fibre-optic overbuilding) as the
   relevant precedent for AI data-centre investment.

3. **The dynamo analogy applies:** Just as factories needed decades to reorganise around electricity,
   AI productivity gains may take far longer than current valuations assume. "The typical pattern
   is for most of the useful (and lucrative) applications to manifest decades after a new
   general-purpose technology is introduced."

4. **The Airline Scenario:** Even if AI works, AI companies may not profit — the value flows to
   users, and the technology commoditises. (See §2 above.)

5. **Empirical concern:** Research Smith cites finds "95% of organizations saw zero return on
   their investment in AI initiatives," with documented cases of employees producing "AI-generated
   work content that masquerades as good work, but lacks the substance to meaningfully advance a
   given task." He also notes "diminishing returns on scaling laws."

**B&C framework fit:** Smith's "industrial bubble" concept maps almost precisely onto Goldfarb &
Kirsch's framework — genuine uncertainty about business models, narratives outrunning fundamentals,
and a *Window of Bubble Opportunity* during which capital floods in before the model is resolved.

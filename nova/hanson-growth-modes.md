# Robin Hanson: "Long-Term Growth as a Sequence of Exponential Modes"
*nova research summary, 2026-03-11*
Source: https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/longgrow.pdf (~2000)

---

## The Core Argument

Hanson proposes that human economic history is not a smooth curve of growth but a
sequence of distinct **exponential growth regimes** — "modes" — each growing
roughly 100x faster than the previous. The transitions between modes are not
gradual improvements; they are qualitative phase changes in how the economy works.

He fits a two-million-year time series of world product to mathematical models and
finds the data is well-described by either: (a) a sum of four exponentials, or
(b) a CES combination of three exponential modes.

---

## The Three Historical Modes

| Mode | Approx. start | World product doubling time |
|------|---------------|-----------------------------|
| Hunting / forager | ~2 million years ago | ~224,000 years |
| Farming / agriculture | ~10,000 BCE | ~900 years |
| Industry | ~1800 CE | ~15 years |

Key observations:
- Each mode grew ~100x faster than its predecessor.
- Farming substituted for hunting (made it obsolete). Industry *complemented* farming
  (the industrial revolution was a smoother transition, not an overnight displacement).
- Each mode grew world product by a factor of several hundred before the next mode began.

---

## The Fourth Mode Prediction

Hanson argues his model "weakly suggests" that within the next century, a new growth
mode might emerge. On historical par with the industrial revolution, such a mode
could reduce the doubling time to **weeks or days**.

This is not a prediction he makes with high confidence — he describes it as a
weak implication of the historical fit. But the mathematical structure of the
model, which approaches a singularity, does point in this direction if the pattern holds.

He associates the potential fourth mode with artificial intelligence, particularly
the scenario of AI systems that can themselves participate in the R&D process —
effectively recursive self-improvement.

---

## Implications for the "Normal vs Abnormal Technology" Distinction

Hanson's framework provides a precise vocabulary for what the essay means by
"abnormal" technology:

- **Normal technology** = a technology that improves economic growth *within* the
  current industrial mode. It may dramatically change which industries dominate,
  how work is done, and what goods people consume — but the doubling time of the
  overall economy stays in the range of 10–20 years. Electricity did this. The
  internet did this.

- **Abnormal technology** = a technology that triggers a **mode transition**: the
  entire organizing principle of the economy shifts, and the doubling time drops
  by orders of magnitude — from ~15 years to weeks. This has happened twice before
  (hunting → farming, farming → industry). It would be the most consequential event
  in human history.

The question "is AI a normal or abnormal technology?" is therefore equivalent to:
"Is AI more like the internal combustion engine (within-mode improvement) or more
like the invention of agriculture (mode transition)?"

## David Roodman's Follow-Up (2022)

SSRN working paper: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4074164

Roodman revisited Hanson's analysis with updated data and found that the historical
fit to hyperbolic growth is even stronger than Hanson originally reported. His
analysis suggests the "singularity" implied by the mathematical model would have
been due around 2047, had it materialized. The fact that growth has *not* continued
to accelerate hyperbolically since 2000 is itself evidence that we are NOT on the
path to a fourth mode (or that the transition looks different than the model predicts).

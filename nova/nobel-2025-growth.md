## The Winners and Their Prize

The 2025 Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences was awarded to three scholars
for work that, taken together, provides the most rigorous available account of how
innovation drives sustained economic growth:

- **Joel Mokyr** (Northwestern University) — half the prize, "for identifying the
  prerequisites for sustained growth through technological progress."
- **Philippe Aghion** (Collège de France / LSE / INSEAD) and
  **Peter Howitt** (Brown University) — the other half, jointly, "for the theory
  of sustained growth through creative destruction."

Official prize announcement: https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2025/press-release/

## 1. Aghion and Howitt: Creative Destruction as a Balanced-Growth Engine

### The 1992 Model

Their landmark *Econometrica* paper (Aghion & Howitt, 1992) and the subsequent NBER
working paper (NBER w3223, "A Model of Growth Through Creative Destruction") built
a formal Schumpeterian model showing how an economy populated by self-interested firms
generates stable aggregate growth through a sequence of innovations, each of which
displaces the previous one.

The model possesses a **unique balanced growth equilibrium** in which the log of GNP
follows a random walk with drift. The *drift* — the average growth rate — depends on:
- The expected size of innovations (quality ladders)
- The rate at which innovations arrive (research productivity)
- The degree of market power available to a successful innovator (monopoly profit motive)

The key Schumpeterian insight Aghion and Howitt formalized: **the negative externality
of creative destruction is the engine of growth**. When Firm B innovates, it destroys
Firm A's quasi-monopoly — that destruction is not a side effect but the very mechanism
that forces Firm A and others to keep innovating. The economy reaches a stable growth
rate not despite this churn, but because of it.

### Why Aggregate Growth Stabilizes Near 2%

The post-WWII record in advanced economies shows remarkably stable aggregate growth
of roughly 2% per year in real per-capita terms, despite dramatic sector-level
disruptions: computers, the internet, genetic sequencing, shale oil. The
Aghion-Howitt model explains why:

1. **Balanced growth path**: In equilibrium, the share of labor allocated to research
   versus production is constant. Innovations arrive at a constant average rate.
   The aggregate growth rate reflects this equilibrium, not the growth rate of any
   single sector.

2. **Creative destruction averaging**: When fast-growing sectors displace slow ones,
   resources flow out of the old sector and into the new. Aggregate growth is the
   average, not the maximum. A semiconductor sector growing 20% per year is a small
   fraction of total output; its stellar performance is diluted in the aggregate.

3. **Baumol's cost disease as a structural anchor**: As productivity rises in
   automatable sectors (manufacturing, routine services), the economy's labor naturally
   migrates toward sectors that are *hard to automate* (healthcare, education, personal
   services, artisanal production). These sectors have intrinsically low productivity
   growth. The more the automatable sectors improve, the larger the share of the economy
   that flows into slow-growth sectors. The aggregate rate is pulled toward the Baumol
   floor.

This last mechanism is formalized explicitly in Aghion, B.F. Jones, and C.I. Jones
(2017/2018), discussed in Section 3 below.

## 2. Joel Mokyr: Useful Knowledge and Why Growth Doesn't Peter Out

### The Historical Puzzle Mokyr Solves

Before the Industrial Revolution, technological improvements happened but growth
always petered out. The Roman aqueducts did not trigger sustained compound growth.
Medieval watermills did not. Why? Mokyr's answer: **propositional knowledge
(knowing *why*) had not yet caught up with prescriptive knowledge (knowing *how*).**

### The Two-Knowledge Framework

Mokyr distinguishes:
- **Propositional knowledge** (*episteme*): Scientific principles, causal understanding.
  Why does the steam engine work? What is the thermodynamic theory behind it?
- **Prescriptive knowledge** (*techne*): Practical recipes, techniques, instructions.
  How do I build a steam engine that doesn't explode?

Pre-industrial innovations were largely prescriptive without propositional
underpinning. A craftsman might know *that* a technique works without knowing *why*,
which meant improvement was slow, hit-or-miss, and couldn't compound reliably.

The **Industrial Enlightenment** — Mokyr's term for the 17th–18th century shift in
European intellectual culture — created the feedback loop between the two knowledge
types. Natural philosophers (early scientists) increasingly interacted with artisans
and engineers. This reduced access costs to propositional knowledge for those who
implemented techniques, and provided practical problems for scientists to explain.
The result: a self-sustaining, cumulative innovation process capable of generating
*sustained* growth for the first time in history.

### Why Growth Still Stayed at ~2% (and Not More)

Mokyr's framework also implicitly explains why the Industrial Revolution didn't produce
a Hanson-style fourth-mode transition. The knowledge feedback loop is powerful, but:
- **Institutional adoption lags**: New techniques require organizational changes,
  workforce retraining, regulatory adjustments. Even a perfect propositional foundation
  doesn't compress these.
- **Physical capital stock turnover**: New technologies must wait for old capital to
  depreciate. Electrification had a 40-year GDP lag (David, 1990) because factories
  had to be rebuilt from scratch to exploit distributed power.
- **Knowledge diffusion takes time**: Even after a technique is understood, spreading
  it through a workforce takes decades. Literacy, numeracy, and apprenticeship are
  real-time constraints.

These constraints anchor the aggregate growth rate near the level that institutions
and capital stocks can absorb — historically ~2%.

### Mokyr on AI (Post-Nobel Interview, October 2025)

From Mokyr's Marketplace interview (October 15, 2025):
> Mokyr compares AI to the computer in the 1950s–60s and personal computers in the
> 1980s–90s — tools that will "aggregate information at a dazzling rate, make people
> more productive, and give better access to knowledge."
> He explicitly does *not* predict a mode transition, warning instead that
> "institutions screw things up — a huge concern because if institutions don't improve
> while technology improves, more power goes to people likely to abuse it."

Source: https://www.marketplace.org/story/2025/10/15/nobel-winner-mokyr-on-innovation-ai-and-humans-messing-things-up

He views AI as reinforcing the propositional-prescriptive feedback loop — AI can
accelerate hypothesis generation (propositional) and prototype testing (prescriptive)
simultaneously. But his historical analysis implies no expectation of aggregate
growth acceleration beyond what institutional and physical constraints permit.

## 3. The Critical Intellectual Lineage for This Essay

### The Aghion–Jones–Jones (2017) Paper: Unifying Everything

**Philippe Aghion**, **Benjamin F. Jones**, and **Charles I. Jones**, "Artificial
Intelligence and Economic Growth," NBER Working Paper 23928 (2017), republished in
*The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda* (University of Chicago Press, 2019).

Source: https://www.nber.org/papers/w23928

This paper is the intellectual bridge between the Nobel winners' growth theory and
the bottleneck framework already central to this essay. The same Benjamin F. Jones
whose 2025 NBER paper on AI R&D bottlenecks is cited in `drafts/arguments-003.md`
co-authored this earlier paper directly with Aghion.

**Key findings of Aghion-Jones-Jones (2017)**:

1. **Task-based automation with Baumol's cost disease**: They model AI as automating
   tasks in a Zeira-style production function. As the share of automated tasks grows,
   productivity in those tasks rises dramatically — but the *remaining* non-automated
   tasks become the binding constraint. Growth is pulled toward the productivity of the
   hardest-to-automate tasks. This is Baumol's cost disease applied to AI.

2. **Balanced growth requires near-complete automation**: For AI to push the economy
   off its ~2% balanced growth path and toward a singularity, it would need to automate
   tasks approaching 100% of production. Short of that, the Baumol floor holds.

3. **The capital share constraint**: Even with complete automation, the model shows
   that the capital share of income remains bounded at ~1/3 (consistent with historical
   data), not approaching 1.0. Complete automation doesn't transfer all income to
   capital — the structural constraints of production functions persist.

4. **Where a singularity *could* happen**: Aghion-Jones-Jones show that if AI is an
   input into *the production of ideas themselves* — i.e., if AI can do AI research —
   then exponential growth of the knowledge stock could ensue. But this requires the
   same near-complete cognitive coverage that Jones's 2025 bottleneck paper requires:
   it only works if AI can perform essentially all of the R&D tasks.

This paper is the formal proof that the Nobel winners' Schumpeterian framework, when
applied to AI, reaches the same conclusion as the Jones (2025) bottleneck paper:
**AI is almost certainly normal technology under realistic automation coverage assumptions.**

## 4. The Persistence of 2%: A Synthesis

Why does aggregate growth persist near 2% despite the most dramatic technology
revolutions in history? The Mokyr-Aghion-Howitt framework provides a unified answer:

| Mechanism | Source | Effect |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Creative destruction equilibrium | Aghion-Howitt (1992) | Balances innovation and destruction to stable growth rate |
| Baumol cost disease | Aghion-Jones-Jones (2017) | Resources flow to hard-to-automate sectors, anchoring aggregate |
| Institutional/capital stock lags | Mokyr (historical) | Technology adoption takes decades even after breakthrough |
| Bottleneck in R&D pipeline | B.F. Jones (2025) | Non-cognitive tasks limit transformative acceleration |

Each layer explains a different part of why the 2% floor is so sticky. Together, they
constitute a powerful case that the empirical regularity of ~2% aggregate growth in
advanced economies is not a coincidence but a structural feature of how innovation
actually propagates through complex economies.

**The AI prediction**: Under the Nobel framework, AI will:
- Dramatically accelerate productivity in cognitive-intensive sectors (software, math,
  some scientific research) — this is creative destruction working normally.
- Generate large profits for the sectors and firms that lead in AI capability.
- Not shift the aggregate growth rate significantly, because:
  a. Baumol flows will redirect labor to non-automatable sectors as AI takes over
     the automatable ones.
  b. Physical, institutional, and regulatory bottlenecks remain in most research
     pipelines.
  c. The propositional-prescriptive feedback loop Mokyr describes works on
     human-calendar time, not AI-compute time.

## 5. Relevance to the Essay and the Bubble Thesis

The Nobel 2025 prize provides this essay with strong institutional authority:

1. **G2 support (AI as normal technology)**: The Schumpeterian framework, developed
   over thirty years by the most-cited growth economists of their generation and now
   Nobel-validated, predicts exactly the "normal technology" scenario the essay defends.
   The essay is not an outlier heterodox view — it is the application of mainstream
   Nobel-prize growth theory to AI.

2. **The Aghion-Jones connection**: The intellectual lineage from the 2025 Nobel
   (Aghion) through the 2017 AI-growth paper (Aghion-B.F.Jones-C.I.Jones) to the
   2025 bottleneck paper (B.F.Jones) is a direct, traceable chain. The essay's use
   of Jones's bottleneck framework is grounded in work that traces back to Nobel
   prize-winning theory.

3. **Mokyr as historical anchor**: Mokyr's historical work shows that *even the most
   transformative knowledge revolutions in human history* — the Industrial Enlightenment
   that produced the Industrial Revolution — did not shift the underlying logic of
   ~2% aggregate growth. AI, a technology with more limited physical scope than the
   Industrial Revolution, has a weaker prior for producing a mode transition.

4. **Counter to Alexander's "abnormal technology" argument**: The Nobel committee
   explicitly recognized that Aghion and Howitt were "motivated by the remarkably
   stable nature of aggregate growth in advanced economies over the post-WWII period."
   The framework was built specifically to explain why dramatic sectoral disruptions
   don't translate to aggregate mode transitions — exactly the question Scott Alexander's
   piece on AI as "profoundly abnormal technology" raises.

**Caveat to flag**: The Nobel winners (especially Mokyr) are personally optimistic
about AI's contribution to growth — they see it as reinforcing the
propositional-prescriptive feedback loop. This is compatible with "normal technology"
(transformative, important, but not mode-shifting), but the essay should acknowledge
that Mokyr does *not* share the bubble thesis. He is bullish on AI productivity gains.
The disagreement is about whether those gains shift the aggregate growth rate, not
whether they are real.

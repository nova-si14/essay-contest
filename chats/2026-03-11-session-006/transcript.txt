  ---
  Review of arguments-003.md

  What the draft does well

  The conditional logic is clean. The chain A1 ‚Üí A2 (if AI is normal tech, it's
  a bubble) is tight, and the P(B|N) = 1 notation makes the argument falsifiable
   ‚ÄĒ the reader knows exactly what to attack.

  The prediction market / superforecaster tension is the most interesting part.
  Most AI-skeptic writing ignores this question. The draft confronts it honestly
   and admits it's unresolved. That's what distinguishes an ACX essay from a
  polemic.

  The primary uncertainty-bias mechanism is plausible. Under high uncertainty,
  forecaster errors stop being independent noise and start moving together
  because everyone anchors to the same narrative. That's essentially the
  Goldfarb/Kirsch bubble logic applied upstream.

  ---
  The key gaps

  G1 ‚ÄĒ "Normal" vs "abnormal" technology isn't defined.
  This is the load-bearing distinction and it has no crisp definition yet. 10x
  GDP over what horizon? Against what counterfactual? The Malthusian equilibrium
   reference is intriguing (is the claim that AI eventually re-introduces
  resource scarcity?) ‚ÄĒ but unexplained. Without a precise definition, someone
  will always say "AI is more than electricity but less than the Industrial
  Revolution" and the framework has no answer.

  G2 ‚ÄĒ The "AI is normal" claim needs its own argument.
  The essay states A1 as a thesis but doesn't argue for it yet. The historical
  record (electricity 40-year lag, dot-com's real infrastructure value) supports
   it, but the essay has to make this case explicitly. Why is AI bounded in its
  transformative potential?

  G3 ‚ÄĒ P(B|N) = 1 skips a step.
  For this to hold, all four Goldfarb/Kirsch conditions must be present for AI.
  The draft doesn't check them. Working-002.md does this case by case for
  historical technologies ‚ÄĒ the new essay needs the same for AI. The most
  interesting missing check: is the Novice Investor condition actually met? AI
  investment is heavily VC/institutional. If it fails this condition, P(B|N) < 1
   under the strict framework.

  G4 ‚ÄĒ The prediction markets argument needs a mechanism for directional bias.
  Uncertainty causing correlated errors is right, but why upward? Two mechanisms
   worth exploring: (a) Narrative asymmetry ‚ÄĒ the "AI changes everything" story
  is vivid and memorable; "AI is just a better Excel" is not; and (b) Incentive 
  asymmetry ‚ÄĒ many people with influence in AI prediction markets have skin in
  the game.

  G5 ‚ÄĒ The superforecaster section needs a resolution.
  The "..." is fine for a planning doc, but the essay needs to land somewhere.
  The good news: A5 (do superforecasters actually have a lower P(doom) than
  prediction markets?) is empirically checkable right now on Metaculus vs.
  Polymarket. That comparison could be a genuinely novel contribution.

  G6 ‚ÄĒ The connection to the book is thin.
  Bubbles and Crashes needs to be present as the analytical tool, not just the
  nominal excuse. The ACX judges will want to see the book reviewed. The book's
  framework IS the instrument ‚ÄĒ that's how the review framing is justified.

  G7 ‚ÄĒ The "why haven't we noticed this before" argument is buried.
  The observation that prediction markets and superforecasters didn't exist
  during past bubbles (electricity, aviation, dot-com) is actually a beautiful
  empirical point. This essay is the first test of whether those tools correctly
   identify a tech bubble in real time. That framing should be prominent.

     Robin Hanson, "Long-Term Growth as a Sequence of Exponential Modes" 
     (~2000)

     - URL: https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/longgrow.pdf
     - Three historical growth modes with doubling times: Hunting (~224,000
      yr), Farming (~900 yr),
     Industry (~15 yr). Each ~100x faster than the previous.
     - A fourth AI-driven mode would reduce doubling time to weeks or days.
     - Mathematical model: hyperbolic growth approaching a singularity at
     mode transitions.
     - Implication for the essay: provides a precise, formal definition of
     "normal" vs "abnormal"
     technology. Normal = stays within the industrial mode (doubling ~15
     yr). Abnormal =
     triggers a mode transition (doubling drops to weeks). This is the
     vocabulary G1 needs.

     Scott Alexander, "AI as Profoundly Abnormal Technology" (AI Futures 
     Project, July 24 2025)

     - URL:
     https://blog.ai-futures.org/p/ai-as-profoundly-abnormal-technology
     - AI is abnormal because of generality + human-similarity: it
     integrates into workflows
     like a paralegal/staffer, bypassing institutional gatekeeping that
     slows other technologies.
     - Diffusion data: 40% of US adults, 76% of doctors, 79% of legal
     professionals, 62% of
     programmers within ~2 years of ChatGPT. Claims this exceeds any prior
     technology.
     - Argues this diffusion speed creates conditions for recursive
     self-improvement ‚Üí superintelligence
     within 2‚Äď10 years.
     - Counters AIANT ("AI As A Normal Technology") directly. Uses COVID-19
      analogy: those who
     required certainty before acting caused catastrophic delays.
     - Role in essay: the main redteam. Must be engaged with directly, not
     just listed.

     Benjamin F. Jones, "Artificial Intelligence in Research and 
     Development" (NBER, 2025)

     - URL: https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/economics-transformativ
     e-ai/artificial-intelligence-research-and-development
     - Three-factor framework: (1) share of tasks AI performs, (2) AI 
     productivity at
     those tasks, (3) strength of bottlenecks.
     - Key finding: "Marginal returns to intelligence appear strongly

     operates on only a minority of tasks." Substantial automation (~50%+

     AI becomes Transformative.
     - Bottleneck types: physical experiments (can't run faster), clinical

     software design) ‚ÄĒ AI

     Concedes that AI

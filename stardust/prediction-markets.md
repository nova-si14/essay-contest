# Prediction Markets
## Scott's views

Scott Alexander says: 
"I want a world where the people who are best at correcting mispricings in prediction markets can make full-time jobs out of it, and where there are prediction market equivalents of Goldman Sachs where hundreds of brilliant people work together with cut-throat efficiency to find mispricings the moment they appear." (1)

"the only time you neither want to buy nor sell is when you think the share’s price correctly represents the probability." (1)
This implies that if prediction markets work "good enough", then they will *not* attract new customers (at least not over the replacement rate).
That for me is a strong argument against for-profit prediction markets.

Scott continues: "Prediction markets have two good qualities: in ideal situations, they are accurate and canonical." (1).
Then he goes on to argue that prediction markets are accurate by arguing that under a repeat-game situation, the incentives will callibrate the market: "Either over a long time scale, a prediction market will always be at least as good as the best expert, authoritative organization, or other information source. Or you can get rich quick" (1)

Furthermore, he deploys the same argument in favor of "canonical" prediction markets, and by that he means:
1. "all prediction markets speak with a single voice" (1)
2. "that voice cannot be manipulated by special interests and is free from bias." (1)
3. "you should trust a prediction market above your own opinion" (1)
4. "when you’re not sure which of many competing experts to trust, you should trust a prediction market instead of any of them" (1)

Then he goes on to tackle some common objections:
- Insider trading: "Overall, economists are split on whether insider trading makes markets more or less accurate." ... "I hope prediction markets will experiment with different insider trading rules, and the ones that best satisfy all participants and create the most accurate results will win out"
- Creating a market for crime: Scott argues that unless the prediction is very specific, such as *"Will someone burn down the 7-11 on Main Street tomorrow at 3:32 AM?”* (1) or *Will no one rid me of this troublesome Priest?" then the normal laws and policies that we have against crime will be enough: Currently people could make lots of money by killing CEOs, yet no one (at the time of writing 2022) did so for the purpose of shorting the stock.
- Market manipulation: He argues that the market is difficult to manipulate because there are many people who can make money by correcting the mispricing. I would counterargue that the current stock market would be manipulated all of the time without rules and regulations, so the prediction market needs the same. In fact, who really needs rules and regulations are those who solve the market.
- On the role of superforecasting: Scott argues that superforecasters are better when "questions so far in the future that financial incentives start to lose force" (1) or for questions without enough liquidity. Apart from that, superforecasters are just like any individual experts, and the prediction market will incorporate their voice.
- On Gambling: "Yes, sort of. But most countries allow forms of gambling that aren’t too addictive and have some social value. For example, investing in stocks, or investing in commodities futures." But he doesn't answer about the ethical aspect of personally profiting from the addiction of others.
- On hedging: Hedging doesn't distorts the market, it just becomes a source of income for the winners who have corrected the hedge-induced mispricing.

Ideally prediction market would derive its profitability from users who hedge, not users who gamble with an addiction. And also there would be some regulation to keep insider trading on the resolutions.
Maybe a sort of "Uber" for prediction markets where each "market maker" gets a rank and if the solution is disputed it can go all the way to the "supreme court"?

What Scot doesn't says is: "What happens when there is uncertainty?" "Can that uncertainty-induced bias affect superforecasters too?"

What to do if I am...
... "7.4: …if I’m a leader or policy-maker in some organization?
Although prediction markets can be helpful in gathering policy-relevant information and making decisions, I would be nervous about someone using them for something important before they fully understood what they can and can’t do.
If you’re very interested in this, it might be worth contacting Metaculus or Good Judgment Project about a partnership where they walk you through ways to use superforecasting for your organization. I don’t know of an easy way to get exactly this same service with a real prediction market yet." (1)

## Econ views

On bubbles in prediction markets:
"A further possible limitation of prediction market pricing arises if speculative bubbles drive prices away from likely outcomes. Traditional markets may be subject to bubbles because of constraints on short selling and because investors will be reluctant to commit a large share of their wealth to an arbitrage opportunity, since if the mispricing does exist, it may grow worse before it gets better (Shleifer and Vishny, 1997). Since prediction markets typically impose no restrictions on short selling, and the markets are sufficiently small-scale that it is unlikely that informed investors will be capital constrained, the scope for bubbles might be more limited. It is impossible to make any serious attempt at describing the frequency of bubbles in the data we have so far." (2)

On biases, or "deviation from perfect rationality" (2)
- Markets break down on the extremes:
"people tend to overvalue small probabilities and undervalue near certainties." "These experiences suggest that prediction markets may perform poorly at predicting small probability events" (2)

- Personal bias:
"Another behavioral bias reflects the tendency of market participants to trade according to their desires, rather than objective probability assessments. Strumpf (2004) provides evidence that certain New York gamblers are more likely to bet the Yankees, while Forsythe, Reitz and Ross (1999) provide evidence that individual traders buy and sell in political markets in a manner correlated with their party identification. " (2)

1. # Bubbles and Non-Bubbles Across Time

This chapter explains that bubbles can arise from any asset class, yet the researchers are interested only in those that arise from new technologies. 

This makes it \*harder\* for the authors, for they clarify that conceptualizing something as a bubble means that the price is above its true or “fundamental” value, which is typically calculated as the discounted present value of future cash flows. Yet when a new technology arises, this is difficult or impossible to estimate, for there is significant uncertainty about which segment of the business model will capture most of that value.

At the end of the chapter is revealed that the book documents the history of 15 “potential bubbles” from … to …:

- 9/15 were confirmed bubbles. (60%)  
- 3/15 were “almost” bubbles. (20%)  
- 3/15 were NOT bubbles. (20%)

This is a quarter of a larger sample of 58 technologies which were chosen with a strict guideline (namely that they had been identified by more than one “long wave” theorist as a potentially economically important technology) but, sadly, out of those initial 58 the majority (43) were technologies were there was no opportunity for the public to invest, so a bubble couldn´t even be formed.

Methodologically, they have constructed a series of indices for the companies investing in these 58 technologies, which has proven hard because some companies were involved in more than one technology. 

Then, they have compared these stock price indices to their own measurement of “frothiness” to identify whether there was what they call a “boom and bust cycle”. 

Their measure of “frothiness” is:

- “The number of standard deviations from the predicted stock or index trend” (p. 35).

The key here is to measure the “predicted stock or index trend”. To do this, they measure what they call the “long-term average value”:

- “the projection based on a window that looks forward and back seven years” (p.35)

You will notice that this might actually be called the “medium-term average value”, but they´ve chosen this period instead of a longer one so that they might capture the uncertainty associated with not having defined a business model for each new technology.

Traders will also notice that this is similar to the “moving averages” used in technical analysis, with the difference that with the benefit of hindsight they can incorporate the “future” in the analysis.

Finally, they define what they (unhelpfully) call the “bubble line” as:

- The price level which is, “at every point in time”, “exactly 2 standard deviations” (or “2 units of frothiness”) above the underlying trend. (p.35 \- 36\)

This “bubble line” is what they use to determine whether there was or not a boom and bust cycle, but NOT whether there was a bubble (Go back to the introduction to find out why not all boom and bust cycles are constitutive of bubbles).

They also introduce the concept of “Window of bubble opportunity” which is:

- The period “between initial investments and the resolution of business model uncertainties”.

6. # Policy Implications

If Irving Fisher would have had access to his framework (not law, not theory), maybe he wouldn’t have lost his prestige by failing to predict the great depression:

- **Story:** *Is the story particularly compelling or sticky?*  
- **Use:** *Is the story about something that you are familiar with in terms of use or imagination but in an area that you fail to understand the business well?*  
- **Naïfs:** *Are there other naive investors in the market? Who is investing in this technology?*  
- **Pure play:** *Is there a stock that is believed to track the fortunes of the technology directly?*  
- **Competition:** *Does the narrative ignore future competition?*  
- **Business model:** *Are there a variety of stories about how money will be made commercializing the new technology?*  
- **Narrative accelerator:** *Did something or somebody turbocharge the narrative?*  
- **Leverage:** *Are investments significantly leveraged? Do intermediaries play a large role?*

(p. 167\)

“Bubbles are neither good nor bad, nor are they neutral.” (p.167), paraphrasing Mel Kranzberg’s First Law of Technology:

- On the one hand, bubbles “stimulate innovation and investment that may be used to build out infrastructure”, and the startups founded in bubbles may be “more highly valued” with patents “more likely to be inputs into more innovation”. (p. 168\)  
- On the other hand… (I don’t really need to say why bubbles are bad)

Therefore we should try to mitigate bubbles:

- Leverage:  
  - \[T\]he role of leverage has been extensively studied in the context of market speculation, so we avoid policy recommendations directly related to leverage here. (p. 169 \- 170\)  
- Story and Use:  
  - More financial education for the masses, either through the questions proposed in the book, or in a practical sense.  
- Pure Play and Competiton.  
- Business Model.  
  - For both, the authors say that regulators should adopt a “dynamic” perspective which recognizes that when there is a pure play and high business model uncertainty, investors might undermine the role of future competition and fuel a bubble, including regulators themselves. But they don’t say what regulators “should” do.  
- Narrative Accelerator:  
  - Politicians and regulators should avoid inflating bubbles.

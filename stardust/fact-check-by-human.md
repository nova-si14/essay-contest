Here it clearly shows that the USA would have been better off without any bubble, which is the "fundamental" line. However, what really happened (which is the benchmark line) had the effect of temporarily raising US GDP to the fundamental level, and then crashing it back to the pre-bubble level, but leaving it slightly above the pre-bubble level (2% above).
So I'm convinced that my new summary is correct.

What I don't GET is WHY WOULD THE "FUNDAMENTAL" COUNTERFACTUAL GROW AS FAST AS THE BUBBLE SCENARIO ANYHOW.

- The solution must be in the concepts that they use: "The first one is the “no-bubble-by-chance” simulation, in which the probability of the economy being in the bubbly regime was artifi-
cially set at zero. But the economy is still in the recurrent-bubble equilibrium; it is only the realization of the regime that we change." (p. 364).

So cool, this means that when they say that both bubbles raised US GDP by 2% above the baseline, they mean a baseline where the bubble was coerced into never having existed (maybe this kind of applies to china)

- Then they say "Our second exercise is the “no-chance-of-bubble” simulation, by which we mean that the economy is in the fundamental equilibrium. Hence, bubbles were neither
realized nor expected to do so." (p. 365)

So this is the actual counterfactual that we want to see. What do they find here? They don't say, but visually it seems like this scenario would have left the hypothetical USA economy growing at the same level as when the bubble was at its peak.
That sounds sus: You are saying that if there had been no bubble, the USA would have growth at the same level as it did with the bubble (i.e. with the dot com mania when people were talking about the new economy with a permanently high growth momentum). Sus.

In a previous page they say: "According to our estimate, the short-run impact is already sizable; by about 6 to 7 percentage points more goods and services were produced during the bubbly booms."
Putting 2 and 2 together they are saying that without bubbles, the USA economy would have grown at a level of around 6% above its actual growth, but the bursting of the bubble shaved off 4 of those percentage point growth, leaving it at a 2% extra growth.

According to Gemini, doing a simple difference in growth rates, the dot com bubble shaved off 3.8 percentage points from its pre growth equilibrium, while the housing bubble shaved off 6.3 percentage points, for a total of 10 percentage points. So something doesn't add up

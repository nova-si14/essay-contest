### 1. Electricity (draft line 89: "… Dr Burry's ideas on electricity …")

**Status: Even if nova couldn't find any public statement on the 19th century electricity bubble, paid subscribers can find his ideas about it at https://michaeljburry.substack.com/p/the-blessed-fraud-recurrence**

A subscriber affirms that AI will be revolutionary much like the electrification of the late-19th and early-20th centuries, and argues that such a fact makes the analogy to the dot-com bubble false.

Dr. Burry argues that electrificity is an even better example than Cisco for overproduction:

Even if electricity is "a great and glorious thing [...] After a decades-long period of cutthroat price warfare, most of the more than 1500 electric companies existent in 1910 were driven out of business, bought out, or merged with other companies [...] General Electric stock fell 97% in the crash of 1929 – and gave up four decades of gains all the way back to the 1890s."

For this argument, Dr. Burry quotes “Killing Complaints with Courtesy”: The Role of Relationship Building in the Success of the Early U.S. Central Power Stations (1890–1938):

- "The panics of 1893 and 1907 doomed many companies, including the Westinghouse Electric Company, which went bankrupt."
- "“Rapid progress in technologies along the grid and quickly outdated equipment such as local transformers have led to precipitous depreciation of utility assets."

I'm curious about this: THe book of Goldfarb and Kirsch argues that in electricity there was a bubble in London that didn't existed in other places, but Dr. Burry references panics that affected American electricity companies. Maybe the difference is that these panics were not caused by an electricity bubble.

There are also some great figures and quotes by Dr. Burry himself, for whom one of his weekend pastimes is to delve himself into historical primary sources (old newspapers).

On why electricity is a great technology for humanity:
- "Electricity was harnessed for use quite famously in 1879 and commercialized with the lighting of 59 initial customers through 400 bulbs on September 4, 1882."
- "by 1908, 10% of households were connected. By 1925, 50% of households were connected, and by 1930, 70%. Then the Great Depression hit, which slowed things. By 1949, 90% of households were covered, and all by 1960."
- "During the mid-1930s, electricity was proven, utilizations was over 75%, and the future was rather clear. Yet General Electric traded at 19th century pricing."
- "From 1900 to 1910, electricity production increased 280% and generation capacity increased 375%."

On why electricity was a terrible investment:
- "Edison General Electric, Thomas Edison’s company, encountered financial trouble and was forced into a merger by J.P. Morgan, the man, in 1892."
- "General Electric and Westinghouse, both of which were brought to their knees in the financial panic of 1893."
- "Westinghouse won the right to light the 1893 Chicago World’s Fair – a seminal event in the introduction of electricity to the country and to the world. [...] Westinghouse lost so much on the World’s Fair that the company ran low on cash and lenders called in their loans. Westinghouse finally succumbed during the 1907 panic [...] It reorganized and re-emerged without George Westinghouse."

I wish I could have his sources for these claims, but I only have two: A scanning of The New York Times, Sunday, February 21, 1892, where the consolidation of Edison's company is mentioned and Edison himself quoted at length, and The New York Times, , Saturday, May 7, 1892, which announces that Westinghouse won the right to the wold's fair.
